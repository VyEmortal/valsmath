3.2 — Indeterminate Forms and L'Hôpital's Rule

In this section, we will discuss a tool to evaluate limits in the indeterminate forms \(0/0\) and \(\pm \infty/\infty.\) These forms of limits are considered indeterminate because further analysis is required to determine their behavior. We will also analyze other indeterminate forms that can be algebraically manipulated to be in those forms.


In section 1.2, we introduced the idea of an indeterminate limit and utilized algebra to evalute examples of it. For example, consider the limit \(\ds \lim_{x \to 3} (x^2 - 9)/(x - 3).\) We obtain the indeterminate form \(0/0\) if we attempt to perform direct substitution; i.e., the numerator and denominator both evaluate to \(0\) if we plug in \(x = 3.\) However, factoring the numerator as \(\ds \lim_{x \to 3} (x + 3)(x - 3)/(x - 3),\) the limit becomes \(\ds \lim_{x \to 3} (x + 3) = 6.\) Algebra permitted us to evaluate this indeterminate limit, but there are some examples of indeterminate limits in which the resource of algebraic manipulation is not available. In this section, we will discuss a systematic tool that permits us to evaluate any limit in the indeterminate forms \(0/0\) and \(\pm \infty/\infty\)—L'Hôpital's Rule.


L'HÔPITAL'S RULE

Let \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) be differentiable and \(g'(x) \ne 0\) on an open interval \(I,\) except possibly at \(x = a, a \in I.\) If \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) and \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\) are both \(0\) or \(\infty,\) then \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}.\)


In words, we are instructed to differentiate the numerator and denominator of a limit in an indeterminate form \(0/0\) or \(\pm \infty/\infty,\) and re-evaluate the limit. The notations "\(0/0\)" and "\(\pm\infty/\infty\)" solely serve as forms to model a quotient of limits, each of which tends to \(0\) or \(\infty.\) These expressions are meaningless on their own, so it is incorrect to state that a limit equals \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty.\) It is also important to note that L'Hôpital's Rule applies for one-sided limits and limits at infinity.


As an example, consider \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} \sin(x)/x,\) which is in the indeterminate form \(0/0.\) We have previously presented a geometric argument to verify that this limit equals \(1\) and used it to prove that \(\ds \sin'(x) = \cos(x).\) Using L'Hôpital's Rule, if we differentiate the numerator and denominator, then we obtain \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin(x)}{x} &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} \sin(x)}{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} x} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos(x)}{1} \\ &= 1, \ea \] as anticipated.


INDETERMINATE FORMS \(0/0\) AND \(\infty/\infty\)

If \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\) is in the indeterminate form \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty,\) then:

\((1)\)
Differentiate \(f(x)\) and \(g(x).\)
\((2)\)
Evaluate \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}.\)
\((3)\)
L'Hôpital's Rule states that \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)},\) provided that the conditions are met.

EXAMPLE \(1\)
For each of I.–V., evaluate the limit or state that it does not exist.
  1. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{2x}{\sin(4x)}\)
  2. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{\ln(x)}{x - 1}\)
  3. \(\ds \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{x + 2}{2 - 2 \cos(\pi x)}\)
  4. \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sqrt{x}}{\ln(x)}\)
  5. \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} |x| \cot(x)\)
  1. Because \(2x\) and \(\sin(4x)\) both tend to \(0\) as \(x\) approaches \(0,\) the limit \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} 2x/\sin(4x) \) is in a \(0/0\) indeterminate form. Because both functions are differentiable at \(x = 0,\) we can apply L'Hôpital's Rule—we differentiate the numerator and denominator. Therefore, \[ \begin{aligned} \underbrace{\lim_{x \to 0}\frac{2x}{\sin(4x)}}_{0/0} &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\frac{\dd}{\dd x}(2x)}{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} \sin(4x)} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{2}{4 \cos(4x)} \\ &= \box{\frac{1}{2}.} \end{aligned} \]
  2. \(\ln(x)\) and \(x - 1\) both tend to \(0\) as \(x\) approaches \(1.\) Therefore, the limit \(\ds \lim_{x \to 1} \ln(x)/(x - 1)\) is in the indeterminate form \(0/0.\) Because these functions are differentiable at \(x = 1,\) we can apply L'Hôpital's rule: \[ \begin{aligned} \underbrace{\lim_{x \to 1}\frac{\ln(x)}{x - 1}}_{0/0} &= \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} \ln(x)}{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} (x - 1)} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{1/x}{1} \\ &= \box{1.} \end{aligned} \]
  3. \(x + 2\) and \(2 - 2 \cos(\pi x)\) both approach \(0\) as \(x\) approaches \(-2.\) Therefore, the limit \(\ds \lim_{x \to -2} (x + 2)/(2 - 2 \cos(\pi x))\) is in the indeterminate form \(0/0.\) Both functions are differentiable, so we can apply L'Hôpital's rule: \[ \begin{aligned} \underbrace{\lim_{x \to -2} \frac{x + 2}{2 - 2 \cos(\pi x)}}_{0/0} &= \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{\frac{\dd}{\dd x}(x + 2)}{\frac{\dd}{\dd x}(2 - 2 \cos(\pi x))} \\ &= \lim_{x \to -2} \frac{1}{2 \pi \sin(\pi x)}. \\ \end{aligned} \] Notice that \(\ds 1/(2 \pi \sin(\pi x))\) tends to \(1/0\) as \(x\) approaches \(-2.\) Therefore, the limit does not exist.
  4. As \(x\) approaches \(\infty,\) both \(\sqrt{x}\) and \(\ln(x)\) approach \(\infty.\) Therefore, the limit \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \sqrt{x}/\ln(x)\) is in the indeterminate form \(\infty/\infty.\) \(\sqrt{x}\) and \(\ln{x}\) for all \(x \gt 0,\) so we can apply L'Hôpital's rule. \[ \begin{aligned} \underbrace{\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sqrt{x}}{\ln(x)}}_{\infty/\infty} &= \lim_{x \to \infty}\frac{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} \sqrt{x}}{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} \ln(x)} \\ &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x} }}{1/x} \\ &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x}{2\sqrt{x}} \\ &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sqrt{x}}{2} \\ &= \box{\infty.} \end{aligned} \]
  5. To consider the limit of \(|x|\) as \(x\) approaches \(0,\) we will rewrite \(|x|\) using a piecewise function. \[ |x|= \bc -x &x \lt 0 \\[2ex] 0 &x = 0 \\[2ex] x &x \gt 0. \ec \] Therefore, \[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{x \to 0^-} |x| \cot(x) &= \lim_{x \to 0} -x \cot(x) \\ \lim_{x \to 0^+} |x| \cot(x) &= \lim_{x \to 0} x \cot(x) \end{aligned} \] These one-sided limits must be equivalent in order for \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} |x| \cot(x)\) to be defined. Considering the right-hand limit, \[ \lim_{x \to 0^+} |x| \cot(x) = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{x \cos(x)}{\sin(x)}, \] we notice that it is in the indeterminate form \(0/0.\) Therefore, applying L'Hôpital's rule, \[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{x \cos(x)}{\sin(x)} &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} x \cos(x)}{\frac{\dd}{\dd x} \sin(x)} \\ &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos(x) - x \sin(x)}{\cos(x)} \\ &= \frac{\cos(0) - 0 \sin(0)}{\cos(0)} \\ &= 1. \end{aligned} \] However, the left-hand limit, \[ \lim_{x \to 0} -\frac{x \cos(x)}{\sin(x)}, \] is \(-1.\) Therefore, because the one-sided limits are not equivalent, the limit does not exist.

It is a common error to confuse the procedure of L'Hôpital's rule with the quotient rule. L'Hôpital's rule requires that we differentiate the numerator and denominator individually—this procedure is NOT the quotient rule. Do NOT use the quotient rule with L'Hôpital's rule.

Additionally, we may need to use L'Hôpital's rule several times before obtaining a determinate answer. We may have to perform several iterations if we continue to get a \(0/0\) or \(\pm \infty/\infty\) form. Therefore, if you use L'Hôpital's rule and still obtain an indeterminate form, then continue to use L'Hôpital's rule until you obtain a meaningful answer.


EXAMPLE \(2\)
Let \(g\) be a twice-differentiable function. It is known that \(g(0) = 1, g'(0) = 0,\) and \(g''(0) = 5.\)
Evaluate \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{g(x) - \cos(x)}{x^2}.\)

Performing direct substitution, we get \[ \underbrace{\frac{g(0) - \cos(0)}{0^2}}_{0/0}.\] Therefore, because the limit is in the indeterminate form \(0/0,\) we will apply L'Hôpital's Rule. Differentiating the numerator and denominator gives \[\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{g'(x) + \sin(x)}{2x},\] for which direct substitution yields \[ \underbrace{\frac{g'(0) - \cos(0)}{2(0)}}_{0/0}.\] Because the resulting limit is still in the indeterminate form \(0/0,\) we must use L'Hôpital's Rule again. Differentiating the numerator and denominator for the second time produces \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{g''(x) + \cos(x)}{2} &= \frac{g''(0) + \cos(0)}{2} \\ &= \frac{5 + 1}{2} \\ &= \box{3.} \ea \]

This example shows that it may be necessary to use multiple iterations of L'Hôpital's Rule before obtaining a determinate answer.


EXAMPLE \(3\)
Let \(f\) be a function with derivatives of all orders that satisfies \(f(x) \gt 0\) for all \(x.\) Let \(k\) be a differentiable function defined by \(\ds k(x) = \frac{4x^2 - f(x)^2}{\sin(x - 1)}\) that satisfies \(\ds \lim_{x \to 1} k(x) = 4.\) It is known that \(\ds \lim_{x \to 1} k(x)\) can be evaluated using L'Hôpital's Rule. Find \(f(1)\) and \(f'(1).\)

Because \(\ds \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{4x^2 - f(x)^2}{\sin(x - 1)}\) can be evaluated using L'Hôpital's Rule, the limit must be in either of the indeterminate forms \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty.\) Since the limit of the denominator, \(\ds \lim_{x \to 1} \sin(x - 1),\) is \(0\) when \(x = 1,\) the limit must be in the form \(0/0\) (this is one of the forms for which L'Hôpital's Rule is applicable.) Therefore, because \(k\) is continuous (being differentiable implies being continuous), \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 1} \left(4x^2 - f(x)^2 \right) = 4 - f(1)^2 &= 0 \\ \therefore f(1) &= 2. \ea \] (Note that \(f(x) \gt 0\) for all \(x,\) so we ignore the negative solution \(f(1) = - 2.\)) Now, applying L'Hôpital's Rule, we differentiate the numerator and denominator to get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{8x - 2f(x)f'(x)}{\cos(x - 1)} = \frac{8 - 2f(1) f'(1)}{\cos(1 - 1)} &= 4\\ 8 - 4 f'(1) &= 4 \\ \therefore f'(1) &= 1. \ea \] \[ \box{ \ba f(1) &=2. \\ f'(1) &= 1. \\ \ea} \]


L'Hôpital's Rule is useful for comparing the relative rates of growth or decay of various functions. Consider two functions, \(f\) and \(g,\) which are both positive. In order to determine which function grows faster, we can consider the limit \(\ds L = \lim_{x \to \infty} f(x)/g(x).\) Analyzing this quotient initiates a battle between \(f\) and \(g\): if \(f(x)\) grows faster than \(g(x),\) then \(L\) will approach infinity. However, if \(g(x)\) grows faster than \(f(x),\) then \(L\) will approach \(0.\) Additionally, if \(L = k,\) where \(k\) is a non-zero constant, then \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) grow at the same rate.


As an example, let us determine which function grows faster: \(f(x) = x^{1/5}\) or \(g(x) = \ln(x).\) We will consider the limit \[L = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^{1/5}}{\ln(x)}. \] We notice that this limit is in the indeterminate form \(0/0,\) so we can apply L'Hôpital's Rule to get \[ \ba L &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{1}{5} x^{-4/5}}{1/x} \\ &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{5}x^{1/5} \\ &= \infty. \ea \] Thus, we conclude that \(f(x) = x^{1/5}\) grows faster than \(g(x) = \ln(x).\) Both functions are unbounded as \(x\) approaches infinity; therefore, as \(x\) approaches infinity, \(f(x)\) tries to make the quotient \(f(x)/g(x)\) go to infinity, while \(g(x)\) tries to make the quotient decay to \(0.\) Because the quotient \(f(x)/g(x)\) approaches infinity, \(f(x)\) overpowers \(g(x),\) forcing the quotient to tend to infinity. Additionally, had we instead let \(f(x) = \ln(x)\) and \(g(x) = x^{1/5},\) then \(L\) would have been \(0\) and we would have concluded that \(g(x) = x^{1/5}\) grew faster.


RELATIVE RATES

Let \(\ds L = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}.\)

(a)
If \(L = \infty,\) then \(f(x)\) grows faster than \(g(x).\)
(b)
If \(L = 0,\) then \(g(x)\) grows faster than \(f(x).\)
(c)
If \(L = k,\) where \(k \ne 0,\) then \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) grow at the same rate.

EXAMPLE \(4\)
As \(x\) approaches infinity, show that \(\ds f(x) = e^{-x}\) decays to \(0\) faster than \(\ds g(x) = 1/x.\)

We can set up a quotient of these two functions, \(f(x)/g(x),\) and take the limit as \(x\) goes to infinity. In this case, if \(f(x)\) decays faster than \(g(x),\) then the quotient \(f(x)/g(x)\) will tend to \(0.\) Conversely, if \(g(x)\) decays faster than \(f(x),\) then the quotient will go to infinity.

We have \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{e^{-x}}{1/x} \\ &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x}{e^x}. \\ \ea \] Because the limit is in the indeterminate form \(\infty/\infty,\) applying L'Hôpital's Rule gives \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x}{e^x} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{e^x} \\ &= 0. \ea \]

Since the quotient \(f(x)/g(x)\) goes to \(0\) as \(x\) approaches infinity, we conclude that \(f(x) = e^{-x}\) decays faster.


Let us now discuss other indeterminate forms of limits, \(0 \times \infty, 0^0, 1^\infty,\) and \(\infty^0.\) These forms can be manipulated into the forms \(0/0\) and \(\pm \infty/\infty\) so that L'Hôpital's Rule can be used.


For the form \(0 \times \infty,\) we will assignate the functions \(p\) and \(q.\) If \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} p(x) = 0\) and \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} q(x) = \infty,\) then \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} p(x) q(x)\) is in the form \(0 \times \infty.\) However, we can rewrite this limit as \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} \frac{p(x)}{1/q(x)},\) which is in the form \(0/0,\) or as \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} \frac{q(x)}{1/p(x)},\) which is in the form \(\infty/\infty.\) These algebraic manipulations force any limit in the form \(0 \times \infty\) into the forms \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty,\) for which we can use L'Hôpital's Rule.


As an example, consider the limit \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0^+} x \ln(x).\) Analytically, this limit represents a battle between \(x\) and \(\ln(x);\) as \(x\) approaches \(0\) from the right-hand side, the term \(x\) forces the product \(x \ln(x)\) to \(0,\) while \(\ln(x)\) tries to push the product to negative infinity. We can rewrite the limit as \[\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\ln(x)}{1/x},\] which is in the indeterminate form \(\infty/\infty.\) Therefore, by L'Hôpital's Rule, we obtain \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1/x}{-1/x^2} &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} (-x) \\ &= 0. \ea \] This result tells us that, as \(x\) approaches \(0,\) \(x\) is the stronger term—forcing the product \(x \ln(x)\) to approach \(0\) faster than \(\ln(x)\) forces the product to \(-\infty.\)


INDETERMINATE FORM \(0 \times \infty\)

If \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} p(x) q(x)\) is in the indeterminate form \(0 \times \infty,\) then:

\((1)\)
Force \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} p(x) q(x)\) into the form \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty\) by rewriting it as the quotient \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} \frac{p(x)}{1/q(x)}\) or \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} \frac{q(x)}{1/p(x)}.\)
\((2)\)
Use L'Hôpital's Rule to evaluate the resulting indeterminate form. Depending on which form is simplest, decide whether to write the quotient to be in the form \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty.\) (There are some instances in which only one form yields a determinate answer.)

EXAMPLE \(5\)
Evaluate \(\ds \lim_{x \to -\infty} xe^{x}\) or state that it does not exist.

We notice that the limit \(\ds \lim_{x \to -\infty} xe^{x}\) is in the indeterminate form \(0 \times \infty.\) However, we can algebraically manipulate the limit into a quotient by writing it as \[ \lim_{x \to -\infty} xe^{x} = \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{x}{1/e^x}, \\ \] which is in the form \(\infty/\infty.\) Therefore, utilizing L'Hôpital's Rule, we have \[ \ba &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{1}{-1/e^x} \\ &= \lim_{x \to -\infty} -e^x \\ &= \box{0.} \ea \]

However, if we wrote the quotient as \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{e^x}{1/x},\] which is in a \(0/0\) form, then L'Hôpital's Rule would give \[\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{e^x}{-1/x^2},\] and further applications of L'Hôpital's Rule would give \(\ds \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{e^x}{2/x^3}, \lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{e^x}{-6/x^4}, \dots,\) leading nowhere because repeated iterations would still be in indeterminate forms. This problem shows that only one choice, writing the quotient as \(\infty/\infty,\) yields a determinate answer.


The last types of indeterminate forms are \(0^0, 1^\infty,\) and \(\infty^0.\) To convert these forms to the form \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty,\) we need to utilize logarithms. Let \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) be differentiable functions, and let \(y = p(x)^{q(x)}.\) If \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} y\) is in an indeterminate form \(0^0, 1^\infty,\) or \(\infty^0,\) then the strategy we will use involves re-expressing \[\lim_{x \to a} y = \lim_{x \to a} p(x)^{q(x)}\] as \[ \ba \lim_{x \to a} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \to a} \ln\left(p(x)^{q(x)}\right) \\ &= \lim_{x \to a} q(x) \ln p(x), \ea \] which can be manipulated into a quotient of the indeterminate form \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty,\) as appropriate.


Consider, as an example, the limit \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0^+} x^{1/x}.\) We notice that, as \(x\) approaches \(0,\) the base tends to \(0\) and the exponent tends to infinity. Thus, this limit is in the indeterminate form \(0^\infty.\) First, let \(y = x^{1/x}.\) Then, \[ \ba \ln(y) &= \ln \left(x^{1/x}\right) \\ &= \frac{1}{x} \ln(x) \\ \lim_{x \to 0^+} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\ln(x)}{x} \\ &= -\infty. \ea \] Therefore, because \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0^+} \ln(y) = -\infty,\) it follows that \(\ds \box{\lim_{x \to 0^+} y = e^{-\infty} = 0.}\)


INDETERMINATE FORMS WITH EXPONENTS

If \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} p(x)^{q(x)}\) is in the indeterminate form \(0^0, 1^{\infty},\) or \(\infty^0,\) then:

\((1)\)
Let \(y = p(x)^{q(x)}.\) It then follows that \(\ds \ln(y) = q(x) \ln\left(p(x)\right).\)
\((2)\)
Consider \(\ds L = \lim_{x \to a} \ln(y).\) You may need to algebraically manipulate the limit into the indeterminate forms \(0/0\) or \(\infty/\infty\) and use L'Hôpital's Rule.
\((3)\)
\(\ds \lim_{x \to a} y = e^L.\)

EXAMPLE \(6\)
Evaluate \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} \Big(3e^{2x} - 2\Big)^{1/({2x^2 - 5x})} \) or state that it does not exist.

This limit is in the indeterminate form \(1^\infty.\) We will let \[y = \Big(3e^{2x} - 2\Big)^{1/({2x^2 - 5x})}. \] Then, \[ \ba \ln(y) &= \ln \left( \left( 3e^{2x} - 2 \right)^{1/({2x^2 - 5x})} \right) \\ &= \frac{1}{2x^2 - 5x} \ln \left( 3e^{2x} - 2 \right). \\ \lim_{x \to 0} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{2x^2 - 5x} \ln \left( 3e^{2x} - 2 \right). \\ \ea \] The limit \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} \ln(y)\) is in the indeterminate form \(0/0,\) so we can apply L'Hôpital's Rule to obtain \[ \ba \lim_{x \to 0} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{6e^{2x} \cdot \dfrac{1}{3e^{2x} - 2}}{4x - 5} \\ &= -6/5. \\ \ea \]

Because \(\ds \lim_{x \to 0} \ln(y) = -6/5,\) it follows that \(\ds \box{\lim_{x \to 0} y = e^{-6/5}.}\)


EXAMPLE \(7\)
Evaluate \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} (\ln x)^{1/x} \) or state that it does not exist.

This limit is in the indeterminate form \(\infty^0.\) First, let \[y = \left(\ln x \right)^{1/x}.\] It then follows that \[ \ba \ln(y) &= \frac{1}{x} \ln \left(\ln x \right) \\ \lim_{x \to \infty} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x} \ln \left(\ln x \right). \\ \ea \] Considering \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \ln(y)\) is useful because it produces \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln \left(\ln x \right)}{x},\) which is in the indeterminate form \(\infty/\infty.\) Therefore, applying L'Hôpital's Rule rule gives \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \ln(y) &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{1}{x\ln(x)}}{1} \\ &= 0. \ea \] Therefore, since \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \ln(y) = 0,\) it follows that \(\ds \box{\lim_{x \to \infty} y = e^0 = 1.}\)


It is important to note that there are some cases in which L'Hôpital's Rule fails. Consider, as an example, the limit \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \cos(x)/x,\) which is in the indeterminate form \(0/0.\) If we attempt to apply L'Hôpital's Rule, then we obtain \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} -\sin(x).\) This limit does not exist because \(\sin(x)\) oscillates and never settles to a final value. Therefore, L'Hôpital's Rule failed to provide us with an answer. Instead, because \(\ds -1 \leq \cos(x) \leq 1, \) we can set up the inequality \[ \ba -1 &\leq \cos(x) \leq 1 \\ -\frac{1}{x} &\leq \frac{\cos(x)}{x} \leq \frac{1}{x}. \ea \] Taking limits, we get \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} {-\frac{1}{x}} \leq &\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\cos(x)}{x} \leq \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x} \\ 0 \leq &\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\cos(x)}{x} \leq 0. \\ \ea \] Therefore, by the Squeeze Theorem, \(\ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \cos(x)/x = 0.\)


As another example, consider the limit \( \ds \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sqrt{x^2 - 1}}{x},\) which is in the indeterminate form \(\infty/\infty.\) If we attempt to apply L'Hôpital's Rule, then we obtain \[\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2 - 1}}}{1} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2 - 1}},\] which is still in the indeterminate form \(\infty/\infty.\) However, applying L'Hôpital's Rule again gives \[\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sqrt{x^2 - 1}}{x},\] which is a repeat of our original limit. This pattern places us in a never-ending cycle of the indeterminate form \(\infty/\infty,\) never leading to a final value. However, using algebra, \[ \ba \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sqrt{x^2 - 1}}{x} &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \sqrt{\frac{x^2 - 1}{x^2}} \\ &= \lim_{x \to \infty} \sqrt{1 - \frac{1}{x^2}} \\ &= \box{1.} \ea \] These examples show that L'Hôpital's Rule occasionally fails to provide an answer, and we therefore cannot always rely on it. It is important to be able to resort to other methods for cases in which L'Hôpital's Rule fails.


SECTION SUMMARY
  • L'Hôpital's Rule states that if \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are both differentiable and \(g'(x) \ne 0\) on an open interval \(I\) (except possibly at \(x = a, a \in I\)), and \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) and \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} g(x)\) are both \(0\) or \(\pm \infty,\) then \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} f(x)/g(x) = \lim_{x \to a} f'(x)/g'(x).\)
  • L'Hôpital's Rule applies to one-sided limits and limits at infinity.
  • Do NOT use the quotient rule when applying L'Hôpital's Rule! Differentiate the numerator and denominator separately—this procedure is unrelated to the quotient rule.

  • Let \(\ds L = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}.\)

    (a)
    If \(L = \infty,\) then \(f(x)\) grows faster than \(g(x).\)
    (b)
    If \(L = 0,\) then \(g(x)\) grows faster than \(f(x).\)
    (c)
    If \(L = k,\) where \(k \ne 0,\) then \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) grow at the same rate.
  • If \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} p(x)q(x)\) is in the indeterminate form \(0 \times \infty,\) then the limit can be rewritten as \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} \frac{p(x)}{1/q(x)}\) or \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} \frac{q(x)}{1/p(x)}.\) The limits of these quotients are either in the indeterminate form \(0/0\) or \(\ds \infty/\infty,\) for which L'Hôpital's Rule can be used.
  • If \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} p(x)^{q(x)}\) is in the indeterminate form \(0^0, 1^{\infty},\) or \(\infty^0,\) then let \(y = p(x)^{q(x)}.\) It then follows that \(\ln(y) = q(x) \ln \left(p(x)\right).\) Consider \(\ds \lim_{x \to a} \ln(y),\) which will be in either of the indeterminate forms \(0/0,\) \(\infty/\infty,\) or \(0 \times \infty.\)
  • Be aware of cases in which L'Hôpital's Rule fails. Ensure that you can use other procedures for evaluating indeterminate limits as a backup.
